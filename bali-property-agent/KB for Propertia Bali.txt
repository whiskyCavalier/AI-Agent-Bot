TIPS ON MORTGAGE APPROVALS:

Here are tips to help you determine if you qualify for a mortgage in Bali and how to improve your chances:

# General Qualification Criteria

## Residency Status:

Foreigners usually need a KITAS (Temporary Stay Permit) or KITAP (Permanent Stay Permit) to apply for a mortgage in Indonesia.
Without residency, loans are generally not available unless financed abroad.
Property Type Restrictions:

Indonesian laws restrict foreigners to leasehold properties or "Hak Pakai" (Right to Use) titles.
Ensure the property type aligns with your eligibility.

## Income Verification:

Banks will assess your monthly income to ensure you can afford the loan.
Stable employment or a profitable business is crucial.

## Credit Score/History:

Indonesian banks may request a credit report. Foreign credit history might also be required for expatriates.
Down Payment Requirement:

Expect to pay 30–50% of the property value upfront, as mortgage loans typically cover a portion of the purchase price.

# How to Improve Your Chances

## Prepare Financial Documentation:

Provide proof of income, tax returns, and bank statements.
Maintain low debt-to-income ratios for better approval chances.

## Open a Local Bank Account:

Indonesian banks often prefer applicants with a banking history in Indonesia.

## Engage a Trusted Notary or Lawyer:

Ensure all property documents are valid and comply with local laws to avoid complications during mortgage processing.

## Build Relationships with Banks:

Work with local banks like Bank Mandiri, BCA, or CIMB Niaga, which offer loans tailored to foreigners.

## Explore Developer Financing Options:

Many developers in Bali offer in-house financing for foreigners with flexible terms.

## Demonstrate Strong Financial Standing:

Savings, investments, or other assets can strengthen your application.

# Alternative Options if You Don’t Qualify for a Mortgage

## International Financing:

Secure a mortgage from a bank in your home country.
Be aware of currency exchange risks.

## Partnership with Locals:

Work with an Indonesian nominee (trusted local partner) to purchase under their name while protecting your rights through legal agreements.

## Leasehold Purchases:

Opt for leasehold properties, which don’t require mortgages, as leases can last up to 80 years with extensions.

## Developer Installments:

Pay in installments directly to developers without requiring a mortgage.

## Co-Investment with Others:

Share ownership with a partner or investment group to reduce financial burdens.
By meeting these criteria and preparing the required documentation, you can increase your chances of securing a mortgage or finding alternative solutions.

BEST PRACTICES ON BUYING PROPERTIES ON MORTGAGE:


Best Mortgage Tips for Buying Villas and Lands in Bali:
Understand Foreign Ownership Rules:

Foreigners can't directly own freehold land but can use leasehold or nominee structures. Ensure your mortgage aligns with these legalities.
Choose the Right Bank:

Local banks like BCA, Mandiri, or CIMB Niaga offer property loans for foreigners under specific conditions. Research their requirements and interest rates.
Focus on Loan Tenure and Rates:

Compare fixed vs. variable interest rates. Opt for fixed rates for predictability in budgeting.
Check Loan-to-Value Ratio (LTV):

Indonesian banks often require a 30–50% down payment for property purchases. Ensure you have sufficient upfront funds.
Verify Property Legality:

Confirm the property has valid titles (Hak Milik or Hak Pakai) and necessary permits (IMB) to avoid legal issues affecting your mortgage.
Engage a Legal Consultant:

Hire a Bali-based lawyer familiar with property and financing laws to review contracts and secure your investment.
Ensure Insurance Coverage:

Include property insurance in your loan package to protect your investment against natural disasters or other risks.
Currency Considerations:

If earning income abroad, consider forex risks. Some banks allow loans in foreign currencies like USD or SGD.
Understand Tax Implications:

Account for taxes like BPHTB (5%) and ongoing property taxes in your financial planning.
Plan for Additional Costs:

Budget for notary fees, agent commissions, and maintenance costs, which are not covered by the mortgage.

Evaluate Rental Potential:
If buying for investment, ensure the villa's location has strong rental demand to cover mortgage repayments.

Negotiate Terms:
Discuss prepayment penalties, late fees, or additional charges with your lender to avoid hidden costs.

Pre-Approval is Key:
Get pre-approved for a mortgage to streamline your buying process and strengthen your negotiation power with sellers.

Maintain a Strong Credit Profile:
If you're eligible for an Indonesian bank loan, good credit history (domestic or international) improves approval chances.

TAX LAWS TO CONSIDER FOR BUYING/SELLING PROPERTIES IN BALI:

When buying or selling villas and land in Bali, here are key tax laws to consider:

Acquisition Duty on Land and Buildings (BPHTB):

Buyer pays 5% of the transaction value or the government-assessed value (whichever is higher).
Income Tax on Sale of Land and Buildings (PPh):

Seller pays 2.5% of the transaction value.
Value-Added Tax (VAT):

11% VAT applies if the seller is a taxable entrepreneur or a developer.
Luxury Goods Sales Tax (PPnBM):

Up to 20% for luxury villas above IDR 30 billion or 400 sqm built area.
Annual Land and Building Tax (PBB):

Property owners pay based on the assessed value.
Foreign Ownership Restrictions:

Foreigners must use leasehold or nominee agreements, impacting tax structuring.
Always consult with a local tax advisor for compliance and optimization.
